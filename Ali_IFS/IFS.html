Internal Family Systems Practitioner

"IFS offers a way to connect with and understand our inner selves. By doing so, we can find healing and wholeness, and live a more authentic and fulfilling life."

-Richard Schwartz

Internal Family Systems (IFS) is a practice that was developed in the 1980s by Richard C. Schwartz. It is based on the idea that the mind is made up of different "parts" or subpersonalities, each with its own thoughts, feelings, and behaviors. These parts can sometimes be in conflict with one another, which can lead to problems such as anxiety, depression, and unhealthy behaviors


In IFS, the practioner works with the client to identify and understand the different parts of their psyche, and to help the client develop a relationship with each part. This can involve helping the client to identify the different parts and to understand their motivations and needs. The practitioner may also work with the client to help them develop a sense of self that is separate from the parts, which is known as the "Self."


One of the key goals of IFS therapy is to help the client develop a healthy, functional relationship with all of their parts. This can involve helping the client to understand and manage the emotions and behaviors of their parts, and to find more adaptive ways of coping with difficult thoughts and feelings. The practitioner may also help the client to identify and work through any traumas or negative experiences that may be contributing to the functioning of their parts.


Overall, the aim of IFS therapy is to help the client become more self-aware, more emotionally balanced, and more able to live a fulfilling and meaningful life. It can be helpful for a wide range of issues, including relationship problems, trauma, anxiety, depression, and addiction.
